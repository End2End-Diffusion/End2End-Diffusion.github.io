On the Geometry of Latent Token Spaces in End-to-End VAEs

Investigating how pseudo-token embeddings organize spatial structure during joint encoder-decoder training.

When a VAE compresses an image into a grid of latent vectors, something interesting happens: those vectors aren't just a flat encoding. They organize themselves spatially, forming a token-like structure. When you train the VAE end-to-end with a diffusion model, that structure changes in ways that are both surprising and revealing.

The Question

Standard latent diffusion models treat the VAE's output as a fixed input—a spatial grid of continuous vectors that the diffusion model learns to denoise. The geometry of this grid is determined entirely by the frozen VAE encoder. But what happens to that geometry when we unfreeze the encoder?

In end-to-end training, the VAE encoder receives gradients from the diffusion loss. It can reshape its latent space to better serve generation. The question we investigate here is: how does it reshape it? What geometric properties emerge when the encoder is free to co-evolve with the generator?

1 This connects to a broader question in representation learning: do jointly-trained systems discover different internal structures than separately-trained ones? The answer, in this case, is yes.

Tokens in Latent Space

Modern VAEs like the one used in Stable Diffusion encode a $256 \times 256$ image into a $32 \times 32 \times 4$ latent tensor. Each spatial position in this tensor can be thought of as a "pseudo-token"—a vector that summarizes a local patch of the input image, analogous to how ViT patch embeddings work.

Pseudo-Token Embeddings

When we say "pseudo-token," we mean something precise. Each position $(i, j)$ in the $32 \times 32$ grid holds a 4-dimensional vector $z_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}^4$. These vectors are not independent—neighboring tokens share information through the convolutional structure of the encoder, creating smooth spatial correlations.

Key Observation

In a frozen VAE, the pseudo-token embeddings are optimized purely for reconstruction. Their geometry reflects pixel-level similarity: tokens encoding visually similar patches cluster together. In an end-to-end VAE, the geometry shifts toward semantic similarity: tokens encoding semantically related regions (e.g., all parts of a face) cluster together, even when their pixel statistics differ.

Spatial Structure

We can measure this shift quantitatively. Define the spatial coherence of a token grid as the average cosine similarity between neighboring tokens:

$$C_{\text{spatial}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{N}|} \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{N}} \frac{z_i \cdot z_j}{\|z_i\| \|z_j\|}$$

where $\mathcal{N}$ denotes all pairs of adjacent tokens. In a frozen VAE, $C_{\text{spatial}} \approx 0.72$. After end-to-end training, this rises to $C_{\text{spatial}} \approx 0.85$—a significant increase that reflects smoother, more semantically coherent spatial organization.

2 The increase in spatial coherence doesn't mean the tokens carry less information. The mutual information between tokens and image patches remains stable. What changes is the structure of the encoding, not its capacity.

But coherence alone doesn't tell the full story. The more revealing metric is the alignment between token neighborhoods and semantic segmentation maps.

What Changes End-to-End

When the encoder trains jointly with the diffusion model, three geometric properties of the token space shift measurably:

  1. Semantic clustering—tokens representing the same semantic category (sky, object, background) become more tightly clustered in embedding space, even across different images.
  2. Boundary sharpening—the transition between semantic regions in token space becomes sharper. Frozen VAEs produce gradual blends at object boundaries; end-to-end VAEs produce cleaner transitions.
  3. Scale sensitivity—the end-to-end encoder develops multi-scale awareness. Tokens from large objects and small objects occupy distinct subspaces, suggesting the encoder learns scale-dependent features to help the diffusion model.

To visualize this, consider a PCA projection of the token embeddings for a single image. With a frozen encoder, the projection looks like a smooth, undifferentiated cloud. With end-to-end training, the same projection reveals distinct clusters that map cleanly onto semantic regions.

Python# Visualize token embedding geometry
import torch
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA

def visualize_token_geometry(vae, image):
    """Project latent tokens to 2D and color by spatial position."""
    with torch.no_grad():
        z = vae.encode(image)  # (1, 4, 32, 32)

    # Reshape to token sequence
    tokens = z.squeeze(0).permute(1, 2, 0)  # (32, 32, 4)
    tokens_flat = tokens.reshape(-1, 4).cpu().numpy()

    # PCA to 2D
    pca = PCA(n_components=2)
    projected = pca.fit_transform(tokens_flat)

    # Color by spatial position
    coords = torch.stack(torch.meshgrid(
        torch.arange(32), torch.arange(32), indexing='ij'
    ), dim=-1).reshape(-1, 2).numpy()

    return projected, coords

Geometry of Alignment

When we add REPA (representation alignment) to the end-to-end training, the geometric changes become even more pronounced. The alignment objective explicitly encourages the diffusion model's intermediate representations to match those of a pretrained vision encoder like DINOv2.

3 DINOv2's representations are known to be highly structured spatially—they produce excellent segmentation maps without any segmentation training. This spatial structure is precisely what gets transferred through alignment.

This has a cascading effect on the VAE encoder. Because the diffusion model is being pushed toward DINOv2-like representations, and the VAE encoder provides the input to the diffusion model, the encoder adapts its latent space to make the alignment objective easier to achieve. In effect, the VAE "pre-digests" semantic structure so the diffusion model doesn't have to discover it from scratch.

The geometry of the latent space is not a fixed property of the encoder. It is a dynamic surface shaped by the demands of all downstream objectives. When those objectives include semantic alignment, the geometry becomes richer, more structured, and more useful for generation.

The alignment loss can be written as a cosine similarity between hidden states and vision encoder features:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{align}} = 1 - \frac{h_\theta(z_t, t) \cdot f_\phi(x)}{\|h_\theta(z_t, t)\| \|f_\phi(x)\|}$$

The critical insight is that $z_t$ depends on the encoder, so gradients from $\mathcal{L}_{\text{align}}$ flow all the way back to the encoder weights, reshaping the token geometry to facilitate alignment at the diffusion model's internal layers.

Practical Implications

Understanding the token geometry has practical consequences for system design:

Practical Notes
  • Latent resolution trade-offs: End-to-end training makes lower-resolution latent grids more viable. A $16 \times 16$ end-to-end grid can match a $32 \times 32$ frozen grid in generation quality, because each token carries more semantic information.
  • Interpolation quality: Smooth interpolation in the end-to-end latent space produces semantically meaningful transitions, not just pixel-level blends.
  • Encoder initialization: The choice of initial encoder matters less with end-to-end training. Different initializations converge to similar token geometries, suggesting a strong attractor in the joint optimization landscape.
  • Downstream tasks: The end-to-end encoder's latent tokens are directly useful for discriminative tasks (classification, detection) without additional adaptation, unlike frozen VAE tokens.

Open Questions

Several questions remain open, and I expect them to shape the next phase of this investigation:

Optimal token dimensionality. Current VAEs use 4 or 16 channels per token. Is there an optimal dimensionality for end-to-end training? The geometric analysis suggests that higher-dimensional tokens might benefit more from joint training, as there is more room for semantic structure to emerge.

Temporal token dynamics. During the diffusion process, the token representations evolve from noisy to clean. How does the geometry of the token space change across timesteps? Early evidence suggests that semantic structure emerges surprisingly early in the denoising trajectory.

Cross-modal tokens. If we extend this analysis to text-to-image models, do the latent tokens develop structure that reflects both visual and linguistic semantics? The interaction between text conditioning and token geometry is largely unexplored.

4 Preliminary experiments suggest that text conditioning creates "attractor basins" in token space—regions that the denoising process gravitates toward given specific text prompts.

These questions sit at the intersection of representation learning, generative modeling, and geometric deep learning. The tools for answering them are becoming available; what's needed is careful empirical work guided by the right geometric intuitions.
